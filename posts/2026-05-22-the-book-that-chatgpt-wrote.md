A few weeks ago, Steven Rosenbaum — a well-known author and filmmaker — published a book called "The TikTok Algorithm."

Shortly after its release, readers started noticing something strange. The book contained citations that didn't exist. References to studies that were never conducted. Quotes attributed to people who never said them.

When confronted, Rosenbaum gave an unusual explanation: ChatGPT did it. He said he had used the AI as a research assistant, and it had fabricated sources without his knowledge. He blamed the tool.

The internet did what it does best. Some people attacked Rosenbaum for being careless. Others defended him, saying AI is unpredictable and anyone could fall for it.

But here's what nobody asked: what if the problem wasn't using AI — but *how* he used it?

## Two Ways to Work with AI

There are two fundamentally different approaches to working with AI.

The first is delegation. You hand over a task and say, "Do this for me." Write this chapter. Find references for this claim. Summarize this research paper. You treat the AI like an employee who needs minimal supervision.

The second is collaboration. You say, "Help me think this through." You debate the AI. You challenge its claims. You ask it to double-check its own work. You treat it like a sparring partner, not a ghostwriter.

Rosenbaum used delegation. He asked ChatGPT to find sources, and he published them without verification. That's not a tool problem. That's a workflow problem.

## The Real Lesson

AI didn't betray Rosenbaum. His own process did.

If you ask any fact-checker, any journalist, any academic — they'll tell you the same thing: you never publish a source you haven't personally verified. This rule existed long before ChatGPT. AI just made it easier to skip that step.

The danger isn't that AI generates fake citations. The danger is that it generates *plausible-looking* fake citations, and busy people don't bother to check.

This is not a new problem. Wikipedia editors have been fighting fake citations for years. But AI makes the fakes harder to spot, because they look just like real academic references — proper formatting, believable author names, plausible journal titles.

## What I've Learned from Building My Own AI Assistant

I spent two weeks building my own AI writing system on a used PC. An Intel i3-4150 processor. 8GB of RAM. A machine most people would throw away.

What I learned is this: AI is not a research assistant. It's a language model. It predicts what words should come next based on patterns in its training data. It doesn't know facts. It knows what facts sound like.

When you understand this, you stop treating AI as a source of truth and start treating it as a thinking partner.

I use AI every day now. But I never ask it "Is this true?" I ask it "What's an interesting angle on this?" and "What am I missing?" and "How would someone who disagrees with me argue against this?"

That's collaboration. That's the right way.

## The Bottom Line

Rosenbaum's story went viral because it confirmed what people fear: AI is dangerous, unpredictable, and will make you look stupid.

But the real story is simpler and less dramatic: if you don't verify your sources, you will eventually publish something false. This was true in 1995, and it's true in 2026. The tool doesn't change the principle.

Don't blame the hammer when you hit your thumb.

And don't blame AI when you skip the most basic step of research: checking whether your facts are real.
